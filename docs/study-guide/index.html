How to actually study

A practical, neurodivergent-inclusive guide built on cognitive science. The core thesis: most students don't have a study problem, they have a method problem — time spent ≠ learning achieved.

Myths that waste time

The most popular study methods are popular because they feel productive, not because they work:

  • ✕Re-reading notes builds familiarity, not memory — it barely beats doing nothing.
  • ✕Highlighting is passive selection, not processing — useful only if you act on what you highlighted.
  • ✕Cramming can help for a test the next morning, but you forget it almost as fast — useless for finals weeks later or next year's foundation. Spacing wins for anything you need to keep.
  • ✕One long session — fatigue degrades encoding; short focused bursts win.
  • ✕Fixed learning styles — matching teaching to a self-reported "style" has repeatedly failed to improve learning (Pashler et al., 2008; the "meshing hypothesis" is unsupported). Preferences affect engagement; using multiple formats strengthens memory for everyone.
  • ✕Studying with your phone nearby — even a silent, face-down phone measurably drains working memory (Ward et al., 2017), and notifications force costly task-switching. Phone-in-another-room is the highest-leverage fix for most students.

The two principles behind everything

principle 01

Retrieval practice

Pulling info out strengthens memory more than putting it in. Testing yourself badly beats reading perfectly.

principle 02

Spaced repetition

Reviewing just before you forget rebuilds the memory stronger; spreading sessions across days and weeks creates durable retention.

Almost every effective technique is a variation of one or both.

The core techniques (14)

Most are strongly evidence-backed; a couple (mind mapping, SQ3R) have lighter support, flagged below.

Active recall / self-testing most effective

Close the book and retrieve from scratch — brain dump, flashcards, recite aloud, past questions. The single most effective method for most students and subjects.

Spaced repetition

Review same day → 2 days → 1 week → 2 weeks; sort into know / shaky / don't-know piles; Anki automates it. Pairs powerfully with active recall.

Feynman technique

Explain a concept simply; where you stumble is your gap — fix it and retry. A whole-topic application of self-explanation.

Cornell note-taking

Cue column + notes + summary. The value is the generative processing (own words, self-testing from the cue column), not the layout itself — mindless copying doesn't help.

Pomodoro

25/5 focus/break cycles. An attention and fatigue tool that protects focus; the learning still comes from active recall during the blocks, not from the timer.

Mind mapping lighter evidence

Radial map of how concepts connect. Keep it fast and rough (it's easy to make something beautiful rather than learned), label the links to force deeper thought, and finish by rebuilding it from memory.

Interleaving

Mix problem types and topics; it feels harder, which is the point — a "desirable difficulty." Not random subject-hopping; learn fundamentals first.

Elaborative interrogation

Ask "why is this true?" for every fact, chaining the answer to what you already know.

SQ3R active reading lighter evidence

Survey, Question, Read, Recite, Review. The full bundle has only mixed support (Dunlosky et al., 2013 rate it low-utility); the proven core is question-generation plus reciting — the leaner "3R": Read → Recite → Review.

The Blurt (brain dump)

Blank page, write everything unaided, study only the gaps. Doubles as a self-diagnostic.

Dual coding

Pair words with a relevant picture, diagram, or timeline — two independent retrieval routes. The real, evidence-based basis for "visual" tools; helps everyone (Paivio; Mayer).

Pretesting

Attempt questions before studying; guessing wrong first primes you to absorb the right answer (Metcalfe; Kornell et al.).

Concrete examples

Pin abstract ideas to two or three real examples from different contexts, then generate your own.

Self-explanation

At each step of a worked example or passage, explain why this step, why now — turns copying into sense-making (Chi et al.).

Matching techniques to subjects

SubjectWhat works (and what to avoid)
MathPractice problems, interleaving, worked examples then redo, Feynman for proofs, an error log. Avoid flashcards and re-reading — math is learned by doing.
SciencesLabeled redrawing, terminology flashcards, "why does this happen?", past papers, concept maps. Best stack: Cornell + active recall + mind map.
HistoryTimelines, cause-effect webs, date and event recall, practice essays, perspective cards. Best stack: Cornell → Blurt → essays.
English / literatureQuote banks, theme and character diagrams, essay planning, read aloud, argument mapping. Avoid passive re-reading; analyze as you read.
Foreign languagesAnki for vocab, shadowing, translate without looking, write sentences in context, immersive listening.
Computer scienceCode from scratch, rubber-duck debugging, hand-trace logic, Feynman for algorithms. Avoid tutorial-watching without coding.
Geography / social studiesMap labeling from memory, case-study cards, cause-effect diagrams, stats recall.
Music / artsDaily spaced practice (consistency > duration), record-and-critique, theory recall, section mastery.
If a technique isn't sticking, swap it: story or image flashcards, outlines instead of mind maps, explain-the-why after problems, open-book-fading recall.

Studying in multiple formats (not "learning styles")

Matching study to a fixed personal "style" doesn't improve learning (Pashler et al., 2008). What does work: match the format to the content — a map wants to be seen, a poem heard — and stack multiple formats for anything important; more encodings means more retrieval routes (dual coding). Treat these toolkits as a menu, not a "type":

  • ✓Visual formats — for structure, relationships, sequence: mind maps, color-coded-by-category notes, sketch-noting, flowcharts, cover-and-redraw.
  • ✓Auditory formats — for language and argument: read aloud, teach or record-and-replay, study groups, spoken Feynman (doubles as active recall).
  • ✓Hands-on formats — for procedures and skills, or when sitting-and-reading stalls: walk-and-review, build models, role-play, standing whiteboard, study games. Handwriting helps because its slowness forces summarizing in your own words — depth of processing, not "kinesthetic."
  • ✓Text-based formats — when precise wording or a written exam matters: Cornell, rewrite in own words, written Feynman, self-built glossaries, timed essays. Make writing generative, not transcription.
The multi-encoding stack (the evidence-backed core): draw it → say it → write an example → teach it.

Neurodivergent adaptations

Standard advice targets a "mythical average student." Treat each profile as a menu, not a prescription.

ADHD

Interest-, urgency-, and novelty-driven attention. Gamify (Anki streaks, timed challenges), body-doubling, short defined sessions (10–15 min), movement integration, anchor habits, micro-task lists, ride hyperfocus, reduce clutter. Avoid long passive sessions — and self-criticism over task initiation; it's neurological.

Dyslexia

Decoding, not intelligence. Text-to-speech (used actively), lecture recordings, dyslexia-friendly fonts, spacing, and cream backgrounds, visual-spatial methods, oral practice, chunked reading, strategic extra time.

Academic anxiety

A threat response impairs the prefrontal cortex. Warm up with known material, label practice "PRACTICE", box breathing (4-4-4-4 — calms acute stress; the "lowers cortisol" claim is overstated), counter catastrophizing with a Blurt, micro-wins, process goals over outcome goals.

Autism

Depth and systemizing; harder context-switching and sensory load. Special interests as bridges, explicit structure, systems and frameworks first, sensory management, written instructions, transition rituals, routine-based scheduling.

Dyscalculia

Number processing, not intelligence. Verbalize procedures, color-code number systems, physical manipulatives, calculators for computation, real-world anchoring.

Building your study system

A system answers three questions: what to study, when, and how to know it's working.

  1. Weekly planning — specific tasks ("active recall cards for Ch. 6"), split into Must Do / Should Do / Want To Do; always start with Must Do. Use "if-then" implementation intentions ("if it's 4pm Tuesday, then 10 Anki cards at my desk") to boost follow-through.
  2. A sample study day — review yesterday (Blurt or flashcards) → Pomodoro deep-focus blocks with real breaks → end with review and set tomorrow's priority. ADHD version: 15-minute blocks, physical breaks, a visible countdown timer, one written micro-task per block.
  3. A spaced-repetition calendar — learn Day 1 → review Day 2, 5, 12, 30 → monthly until the exam. A "review" = 10 flashcard reps, a Blurt, a Feynman, a past question, or teaching it.
  4. Protect your attention — phone in another room (not just face-down), notifications off, no phone on breaks; single-task — one tab, one task; switching is costly. The highest-leverage habit for most students.
  5. Know if it's working — the only reliable test is producing info without notes (knowledge = retrieval). Beware the fluency illusion: re-reading feels easy and your brain mistakes that for mastery — judge by evidence, not by feel. Weekly Blurt test, monthly past-paper scoring under real conditions, the teaching test. Adjust the technique, not your self-verdict.

Rest & wellbeing

Sleep is studying — the rewiring that turns today's practice into lasting memory happens largely during sleep (REM and slow-wave), so a short night discards part of what you studied; 7 hours beats 2 extra tired hours. Non-negotiable foundations: 7–9 hours of sleep, eat before studying, real breaks, weekly exercise — even 20-minute walks aid consolidation. These are cognitive-performance strategies, not soft self-care.

Key takeaways

  • Studying is a method problem, not a time or willpower problem — time spent ≠ learning.
  • Retrieval practice + spaced repetition underpin nearly every effective technique.
  • Active recall (especially the Blurt) is the highest-leverage habit; familiarity is not knowledge — that's the fluency illusion.
  • Match techniques to the subject; math and CS reward doing, not watching or re-reading.
  • Forget "learning styles": match the format to the content and multi-encode (dual coding) — for everyone, not a "type."
  • Protect attention — phone in another room is one of the biggest wins; single-task.
  • Neurodivergent learners need adaptations that work with their neurology — menus, not prescriptions.
  • A real system = weekly specifics + Pomodoro days + a spaced-review calendar + honest retrieval checks; sleep, breaks, and attention are part of the method.
