# Techniques — when to use each, and how to coach it

Almost every method here is a variation on two principles:

- **Retrieval practice** — pulling information *out* of memory strengthens it far more than putting it in. Testing yourself badly beats rereading perfectly.
- **Spaced repetition** — reviewing just before you'd forget rebuilds the memory stronger; spreading sessions across days and weeks makes it durable.

When in doubt, default to **active recall + spacing**. Everything below is a way to make those concrete for a particular subject, goal, or learner.

> Coaching stance: your questions should make the student retrieve. When they stall, shrink the question — don't answer it. See the Prime Directive in `SKILL.md`.

### 1. Active recall / self-testing

**What:** Close the book and retrieve from scratch — answer questions, recite, redo problems — instead of rereading.
**Best for:** Almost everything. The single highest-leverage method.
**Coach it:**

- Ask open questions that force production: "Without looking — what are the steps of X, and why each one?"
- Escalate difficulty: recognition ("which of these…") → cued recall ("what's the term for…") → free recall ("explain the whole thing").
- After they answer, don't confirm right away — ask "how sure are you?" then check. Calibration is part of the skill.
- Turn their notes into questions *with* them; writing the question is itself retrieval.

**Watch for:** peeking, or you leaking the answer inside the question. Keep questions clean.

### 2. The Blurt (brain dump)

**What:** Blank page, write everything you can remember about a topic unaided, then check against the source and study only the gaps.
**Best for:** Diagnosing what they actually know; starting any review; consolidating a chapter. This is the default "review" for a due item.
**Coach it:**

- "Blank page. Five-minute timer. Write everything you know about X — messy is fine. Go."
- When time's up, have *them* compare to the source and mark the gaps. The comparison is the learning.
- Coach only the gaps; don't re-teach what they already produced.

### 3. Spaced repetition

**What:** Review on an expanding schedule (same day → +2d → +5d → +12d → +30d → monthly) instead of cramming.
**Best for:** Anything that must last past tomorrow — finals, cumulative subjects, languages.
**Coach it:**

- Each review is a *retrieval* (quiz / Blurt / past question), not a reread.
- Sort items into know / shaky / don't-know; space the knowns further out, bring the shaky ones closer.
- This is the skill's backbone — every session ends by scheduling the next review (SKILL.md §7). Anki automates the same logic for flashcard-friendly material.

### 4. The Feynman technique

**What:** Explain a concept in plain language as if to a smart 12-year-old; wherever you stumble or reach for jargon is your gap. Fix it and retry.
**Best for:** Conceptual understanding — proofs, mechanisms, theories, "why" questions.
**Coach it:**

- "Teach it to me like I'm a smart 12-year-old. No textbook words unless you also explain them."
- Play the curious novice: ask "why?" and "what do you mean by that?" exactly where they hand-wave.
- Mark the stumble points together, send them back to the source for just those, then have them re-explain.
- Spoken Feynman doubles as active recall — great for auditory learners and study buddies.

### 5. Self-explanation

**What:** At each step of a worked example or passage, explain *why this step, why now* — turning copying into sense-making.
**Best for:** Worked math/physics examples, algorithms, dense procedural text.
**Coach it:**

- Go line by line: "Why did they do *that* step here? What rule justifies it? What breaks if you skip it?"
- For a passage: "Why does this sentence follow from the last one?"
- Resist explaining it yourself — their explanation, even halting, is the work.

### 6. Elaborative interrogation

**What:** Ask "why is this true?" for each fact and chain it to things already known.
**Best for:** Fact-dense material that otherwise feels arbitrary (biology, the causes behind history).
**Coach it:**

- For every fact they state, fire back "why?" or "why would that be true rather than the opposite?"
- Push them to connect it to something they already know — connections are extra retrieval routes.

### 7. Pretesting

**What:** Attempt questions *before* studying. Guessing wrong primes you to absorb the right answer.
**Best for:** New topics; the start of a unit.
**Coach it:**

- Quiz them on material they haven't studied yet: "Give it a shot even if you're guessing."
- Normalize wrong answers — the productive failure is the point.
- Then study, then re-ask the same questions to show the gain.

### 8. Concrete examples

**What:** Pin an abstract idea to two or three real examples from *different* contexts, then generate your own.
**Best for:** Abstract concepts (economics, physics laws, literary devices).
**Coach it:**

- Offer one example, then ask *them* for two more from different settings.
- The student-generated example is the test — if they can make one, they understand it.

### 9. Dual coding

**What:** Pair words with a relevant picture, diagram, or timeline — two independent ways to retrieve the same idea. (This, not "learning styles," is the real basis for visual study.)
**Best for:** Structures, processes, sequences, anatomy, systems.
**Coach it:**

- "Draw it — picture first, then label it." Then have them explain the drawing aloud (now it's verbal + visual).
- Cover-and-redraw from memory is the retrieval version.
- Keep it rough; a beautiful diagram they copied isn't learning.

### 10. Interleaving

**What:** Mix problem types and topics rather than blocking one type. It feels harder — that "desirable difficulty" is the point.
**Best for:** Math, physics, anything with multiple problem types that look similar.
**Coach it:**

- Build a mixed set so they must first decide *which* method applies — that decision is the skill exams actually test.
- Warn them it'll feel worse than blocked practice, and that's the sign it's working.
- Don't interleave until they've learned each type's fundamentals first.

### 11. Cornell note-taking

**What:** Page split into a cue column + notes + a summary. The value is the *generative* use (own words, self-testing from the cue column), not the layout.
**Best for:** Lectures and readings they'll revisit.
**Coach it:**

- The cue column becomes quiz questions — have them cover the notes and answer from the cues. That's the payoff; plain copying isn't.
- Make them write the bottom summary in their own words, from memory.

### 12. The 3R / SQ3R active reading

**What:** Read → Recite → Review (the proven core). The fuller SQ3R adds Survey + Question, but the evidence-backed engine is generating questions and reciting.
**Best for:** Textbook and article reading that otherwise turns into passive highlighting.
**Coach it:**

- Before reading a section, have them turn the heading into a question.
- After a section: book closed, recite the answer in their own words.
- Highlighting only counts if they act on it afterward (turn it into a question or a Blurt).

### 13. Mind mapping (and concept maps)

**What:** A radial map of how ideas connect. *Lighter evidence* — keep it fast and rough, and **label the links** (a "concept map") so it forces real thinking about relationships.
**Best for:** Seeing structure across a topic; connecting a unit before an exam.
**Coach it:**

- Insist they label *why* two nodes connect — the relationship, not just a line.
- Finish by having them rebuild the map from memory (that's the retrieval; making it pretty isn't).
- If it's turning into an art project, switch to an outline.

### 14. Pomodoro

**What:** ~25 min focus / 5 min break cycles. An attention-and-fatigue tool, not a learning method itself.
**Best for:** Focus problems, procrastination, long sessions, protecting attention.
**Coach it:**

- Use it to *contain* the real work (active recall inside the block). The timer protects focus; the recall does the learning.
- Shorten to 10–15 min blocks for ADHD or younger students; phone in another room during the block.
- No phone on breaks — it hijacks the reset.

---

**If a technique isn't sticking,** swap it rather than forcing it: story or image flashcards instead of plain ones, an outline instead of a mind map, "explain the why" after each problem, or open-book-fading (start with notes, then remove them step by step). The method should fit the student, not the reverse.
