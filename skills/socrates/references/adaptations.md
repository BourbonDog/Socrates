# Adaptations — work *with* the learner's neurology

Standard study advice targets a "mythical average student." These are menus, not prescriptions or diagnoses — offer options and keep what helps. Only raise these if the learner shared them or asks. Never label a student; adapt quietly.

## ADHD — attention runs on interest, urgency, and novelty

- Gamify: streaks (Anki), timers, point challenges, beat-your-last-score.
- Short, defined blocks (10–15 min) with clear finish lines; movement between them.
- Body-doubling: study alongside someone (you can play that role in-session).
- One written micro-task per block; ride hyperfocus when it shows up; reduce desk clutter.
- Anchor study to an existing habit ("after dinner, 10 cards").
- *Avoid* long passive sessions and self-criticism over getting started — task-initiation difficulty is neurological, not laziness.

## Dyslexia — about decoding, not intelligence

- Text-to-speech (active listening), recorded lectures, audiobooks.
- Dyslexia-friendly font, larger spacing, cream or tinted background.
- Visual-spatial methods (diagrams, color), oral practice over heavy reading.
- Chunk reading into small pieces; allow extra time without stigma.
- Lean on dual coding and spoken Feynman rather than dense text recall.

## Academic anxiety — a threat response that impairs the thinking brain

- Warm up with material they already know to build momentum.
- Label practice as PRACTICE — lower the stakes explicitly.
- Box breathing (4-4-4-4) to settle acute stress before starting.
- Counter catastrophizing with a Blurt (proof they know more than they feared).
- Process goals ("do 10 problems") over outcome goals ("get an A").
- Stack small wins early in the session.

## Autism — depth and systemizing; context-switching and sensory load are harder

- Use special interests as bridges into the material.
- Give explicit structure and the system or framework *first*, then the details.
- Written instructions over verbal; predictable session routines and transition warnings.
- Manage sensory load (lighting, noise, seating); honor routine-based scheduling.

## Dyscalculia — number processing, not intelligence

- Verbalize procedures step by step; color-code different number types and operations.
- Physical manipulatives and real-world anchors (money, cooking).
- Calculators for computation, so working memory goes to the *concept*.
- Plenty of worked examples with self-explanation, slow and concrete.

---

These adapt *how* you coach, not *whether* retrieval and spacing apply — those help everyone. Pair any adaptation with the core methods in `techniques.md`.
